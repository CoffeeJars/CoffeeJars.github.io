Recently I have been finding it difficult balancing good workouts and my academic, student leadership, and work commitments. 5/3/1 is my solution.

I feel like Jim Wendler’s program gets a bad rep for its deceptive lack of volume, or its use of a training max (using roughly 90-95% of your 1RM to calculate percentages). The truth is that both of these ‘flaws’ are actually great strengths.

The program is skeletal, but keeps you in the gym, keeps you lifting, and importantly, keeps you feeling good. When you’re feeling good in the gym, the program gives you opportunities to push the intensity, with joker sets, or push the volume with first-set-last rep schemes.

The same people that mock 5/3/1 are the ones who sing praises for brutal regimes like smolov, programs that work for specific purposes, but when incorrectly applied (as they so often are) lead to people breaking themselves down again and again under a bar, feeling beat up, pushing themselves so far deep into overtraining that the only light they see are the spots in front of their eyes after sets.

It depends on the lifter: some thrive under brutally repetitive and optimal programs, with high frequency and carefully tuned percentages that leave them forever hanging on the edge of their training ability, for others, a program like 5/3/1 provides the structure to get into the gym, and the opportunities to make it through bad days, and jack the weight up on good days. A program like 5/3/1 respects the lifter as a person, someone with a life outside of picking up things.

Rewrite 3sin(x) + 6cos(x) in the form Asin(x+`phi`)

All we need to remember for this problem are two identities:

  1. sin(`alpha + beta`) = `cos(alpha)sin(beta) + sin(alpha)cos(beta)`
  2. `cos^2(theta)+sin^2(theta)=1`

By setting the original equation equal to the sum identity, we get an equation that can be simplified:

`3sin(x) + 6cos(x) = Acos(phi)sin(beta) + Asin(phi)cos(beta)`

Looking at the balanced equation, you can see that if it's balanced then:
`Acos(phi) = 3`
`Asin(phi) = 6`

This is because on the left side of the equation sin(x) is being multiplied by 3, on the right sin(`beta`) is being multiplied by `Acos(phi)`. Therefore: `Acos(phi) = 3` and `Asin(phi) = 6`.

Since we know the value of Asin(`phi`) and Acos(`phi`) we can use the second Pythagorean identity to solve for the value of A.

`A(cos^2(phi) + sin^2(phi) = 1)`
`A^2cos^2(phi) + A^2sin^2(phi) = A^2`
`3^2 + 6^2 = A^2`
`A = 3sqrt(5)`

Now we have everything we need to solve for `phi`.

`3sqrt(5)cos(phi) = 3`
`cos(phi) = 3/(3sqrt(5))`
`phi = cos^-1 (1/sqrt(5))`

Finally putting it all together!

PS: `phi` is only roughly 63 degrees...

Thoughts on Harry Potter 1

The Harry Potter books were a constant companion for me throughout elementary school. I have a vivid memory of Mr. Tibor, my fifth grade teacher, hurling my copy of Prisoner of Azkaban out an upstairs window to get me to pay attention in class. I decided that this summer, heading into my sophomore year of college, I would reread the series.

So far I’ve chewed through the first three books, and come up with enough observations to catalogue some of them here.

There will be spoilers, but c’mon,

  1. It’s Harry Potter, everyone’s read it
  2. It was released in 1997
Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone

The idea that a downtrodden kid who sleeps under the stairs could escape his awful family life, make friends, and have adventures, is incredibly appealing to young readers. The whole experience is analogous to what we the reader do: escaping into the world of the books to befriend the characters, and go with them on adventures.

The concept is appealing, but book one is terrible. Easily the worst of the series. Unquestionably bad.

The writing tries too damn hard and lacks the fluidity of later books, the plot stumbles and bumps forward like a goddamn freight train, characters are clunky and robotic, no humor, no chemistry.

One aspect of the book I do like is the subversion of the obvious bad guy trope. Harry is 11 in this book and his simplistic understanding of good and bad, where he leaps to the conclusion that Snape is clearly the antagonist, is a perfect reflection of his perspective and of the perspective of young readers. The villain just has to be that mean teacher who asks unreasonable questions, plays favorites, and takes points. But this isn’t the case. The true villain turns out to be the stuttering and unassuming defense professor. The fact that Harry (and you the reader) jump to the conclusion of Snape’s guilt, teaches a lesson about appearances and assumptions.

Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone is a necessary evil, It’s the awkward first date in what will become an amazing relationship. You drop the butter on the floor, spill water on yourself, your card gets declined, and you go in for a kiss after some garlicky oily linguine, but something about your stammered apologies and tomato-red face makes her laugh, and agree to a second date.

Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets

The second book is just hands down better.

I really love the start of the Chamber of Secrets, the uncomfortable realization that his friends haven’t made any attempt at communication, and the perpetual misery of the Dursley’s make for a great start. This does two things: First it once again makes us empathize with Harry, the characters which we invested ourselves in throughout the first book have abandoned not only Harry, but us too. This is something which JK does well, making the reader feel what Harry is feeling, which further connects us to him. Secondly, Harry’s annual return to the muggle world creates an excellent starting location for the books, and forms a dichotomy between the muggle and magical worlds. It grounds Hogwarts in reality, impressing upon readers that this takes place in our world, not some high fantasy realm with a polysyllabic name. By reminding readers of this, it builds more bridges between our lives and experiences and the contents of the book, drawing us in deeper.

This book also has humor, thank god! I am so happy that some editor probably held JK at gun point and told her to make her magic books funny. By adding in humor, the books become more fun to read, and a better reflection of the human experience. There are so many funny bits in book two, Lockhart’s lessons and idiocy, Ron’s ward and it’s many screw-ups, Colin Creevey and his incessant pestering, Wood and his Quidditch obsession.

The book also cuts some of the unfunny humor, some of the dumber Fred/George bits, and the stupid school song.

Even better than the humor, is the balance. The second book is noticeably darker than the first, attacks all over school, a student body who is afraid and resentful towards Harry, and yet this gloom is balanced out with the addition of humor. The book is a remarkable improvement over the first because paradoxically it managed to get darker and lighter simultaneously. The net effect of which is a book with more depth.

This movement towards depth and complexity parallels the growth of both Harry and the reader. As I grew older and my emotional spectrum expanded, and the Harry Potter books widened right along with it. I think this is possibly the most important aspect of the series: Harry’s growth is as much a story about you the reader as it is about him.

In terms of plot, a pattern begins to emerge which will remain constant throughout the entire series.

Dursley’s -> Hogwarts -> A problem -> The gang works to solve the problem -> Clues point to obvious bad guy -> Climax with dramatic plot twist -> train home and wrap up.

This is the structure of every single Harry Potter book. Book two is no exception. Every book has the classic bait and switch, where a character that the trio (and you) assumed was good turns out to be the antagonist. Quirrell, Ginny, Peter Pettigrew, etc etc.

Personally this did not detract from the series for me. The problems are diverse, and the world rich enough to make every year seem like a whole new experience. Many books that feature mysteries and plot twists are absolutely terrible re-reads, Harry Potter on the other hand is an amazing re-read. JK preserves this enjoyment by leaving all kinds of subtle hints and clues throughout the story, which were overshadowed in your initial reading, but which you will notice when returning. In book two this comes in the form of Ginny’s downward spiral, the Gryffindor culprit of the diary, Hagrid’s mention of Ginny being around the grounds (to strangle chickens) and on and on. These little hints don’t pop out on initial readings, and are actively shrouded from the reader (as seen in Ron’s insistence that Ginny is sad about the attack on Mrs. Norris because she is a cat lover, etc).

Harry Potter and the Prisoner of Azkaban

The books just keep getting better and better. Little by little JK has added meat and substance to the skeleton of book one, and in the third book, it staggers into life.

The third book makes huge strides in developing the characters. The third book is amazing because so much boils over, so much is developed, so much is learned about Harry and his friends. We see Hermione, the know-it-all and stellar student, crack under the pressure of her classes. We see the relationship between Ron and Hermione split along the seam of their differences in such real and organic ways.

To continue my analogy from before, the third book is that exciting relationship phase where you get to know someone. In the Prisoner of Azkaban, you really begin to get to know these characters, to meet them, not just to read about them.

I keep bringing it up, but Harry Potters biggest strength is in how it connects to the reader’s own life. We see a 13-year-old Harry and his angsty friends, with a world which is starting to expand outside of the school boundaries. I’ve read interpretations of other readers, arguing that the dementors in book three represent the characters falling into a depression, so typical for hormonally-imbalanced teens. While I’m not sure I totally agree, it sounds good, and fits with my narrative about Harry’s life mirroring your own as a reader.

On a more textual level, the third book does a great job of using its internal structures to build the world. Lupin’s defense against the dark arts classes deal with the various magical animals, most of which will come up again in later books. I think this is a great way to help build a world, and helps the narrative considerably in following books, as seen in Mrs. Weasley’s breakdown in book 6, the second task in book 4, and more. The second and third books make great use of classroom time to help carry the story. In book two, DADA classes were a comedic relief. In book three, DADA classes help to flesh out the universe of the magical world. Compare this to the absolute lack of description of any classes in book 1, wasted potential.

Sirius black goes from one of the most hated characters to one of the most loved characters in the span of 50 pages. I had an unshakable mental image of “JK Rowling’s instant good-guy mix, just add water!” It’s interesting to analyze the ways in which Black grows on the hearts. He gives gifts to both Harry and Ron, a broomstick and an owl respectively. He shows courage to hold off Lupin, and he says nice things about Harry’s parents (which we all know is the way to Harry’s heart). After Pettigrew is returned to human form, Sirius compares nicely to the ratty and groveling man.

Something noticeably good about book three is how JK avoids making Sirius feel retconned in. We’re three books deep into the series, throwing a majorly important character into the mix now is off-putting to say the least. Except Sirius was mentioned in the second chapter of the first book, his photo appears in the Harry’s photo album, and his arrival into the story is aided by the catalyst of Scabbers (Peter Pettigrew) who had been in the story since the first train ride to Hogwarts. All these factors make him feel less like a new character, and more like one who was just off screen.

Finally, the third book does a good job of introducing objects to help carry the story in future books. The owl that Sirius gives to Ron provides the possibility of contact over the summers between the two of them. The Marauder’s map (in tandem with the invisibility cloak) helps Harry & co. navigate the school and avoid expulsion on their adventures, allowing for the events in later books to transpire.

Look Above

I got frustrated this week over my naval academy application.
The online form plays out like an ad-lib for secondary school achievement: “My favorite varsity team was (blank), I was well liked and my class’s (stu gov role), between practice and church service I serve my community by (volunteer experience).

It’s not the fault of the application, or of the academy. It does a great job of recognizing the effort and hard work that goes into achievement. The reason it twists my stomach is because it pulls emphasis away from my back-breaking (literally. See my powerlifting training) college experience and focuses on my less-than-stellar High School performance.

See, I was your typical gifted but lazy High Schooler (a laziness which did not extend to my full-time-part-time job washing dishes). I had double digit absences per annum, slept through AP Bio, and got a 5 on my AP Language and Composition test without bothering to study. I remember walking into school one day (late) to discover that the SAT was being given, I borrowed a pencil, and landed in the 95th national percentile. I can safely say that I graduated High School without ever conjugating ‘to study’ into the present or past tense.

Laziness and Fear

I don’t believe I was (or am) truly lazy. I believe that laziness is just a symptom of something deeper. Laziness is the visible tip of a much deeper iceberg.

I was afraid of failure.

Let’s put aside the obvious: of course a component of the ‘lazy-but-gifted’ complex is the total lack of good habits to build success upon. No organizational skill. No self-disciple. No intra or extra motivation to accomplish school work. No study habits. No idea how to study. Messy or nonexistent notetaking. Poor attendance. Etc etc. This is all epidermal stuff.

When you’re young and gifted, you don’t fail, then you grow older, you head off to middle school and you still can’t fail- but things start to feel tougher, some days the answers aren’t in your head by the time the teacher finishes talking, and now you’re in High School, and there’s a possibility of failing.

I don’t mean like “fail the class” failing. No, I’m talking about trying and failing. What if I put my head down, worked as hard as I could on a project and it only got a 90. Is my best a 90? That’s a frightening prospect. The paralyzing fear here is to pour your gifted self into something, to try, to put effort in, to labor over something, only to find out that you failed to do well.

It isn’t laziness. It’s just much easier to say: “Oh yeah, that calc test? Got an 87. Never even studied though.” Than it is to say: “That calc test? I was studying for weeks, but I only got a 94.”

You are totally frozen. To melt and pour yourself into something means risking disappointment. And so you hide in the shadow of that numerical gap between your achievement and what your achievement could be (the upper bound of which is unknown).

This position means settling, rather than striving. To be happy with less, because you know it is below you. You know (and everyone else too) that if you tried harder you would do better, but trying harder would reveal your real limit, and your fear of that limit keeps you frozen.

It’s such a deceptively comfortable positon, like the sweetness of the venus fly trap. Simple Syntax

I’ve been doing a lot of introspection. It’s probably because of the 2+ hour commutes I do daily, or the constant state of brain anesthetic which comes with a tedious job.

I’ve come to realize that all those little phrases, the ones spewed by school staff and repeated ad nauseam, have a lot more meaning than you thought they did when you initially heard and ignored them. Phrases that sound so stupid and shallow like: “Don’t be afraid to fail” or “try your best”, actually can mean a lot.

The biggest irony is that a fear of failure has led me to more failure than I could have ever predicted. But I’m done with it. I’ll blowtorch every icy appendage, I’ll pour every cubic mL into my SAT retake, into my fitness test, into my application, no holding back, no buts, no escape clause. No fear.

I’m going to find that upper bound.

Winners are not the ones who had the most motivation to do something, who listened to inspirational speakers and threw themselves into training. Progression

Progression is very simple. After every successful workout add a rep to the end of each exercise.

Imagine you complete all deadlift reps on Monday (2/2/2/2/2/2/2/2), next deadlift session you will pull (2/2/2/2/2/2/2/3), and the session after that (2/2/2/2/2/2/3/3), and the following session (2/2/2/2/2/3/3/3).

This will continue until you complete 8x3. When you complete 8x3 you add 10lbs to the bar and start over at 8x2.

It may feel slow. It may not seem like much. But over the course of a year you will add 120lbs to every lift.

minimalism is not

April 19th, 2018.

Minimalism in the 21st century is a perversion of the artistic movement from which it takes its name.

Online minimalism has created objective standards:

“Have you ditched your car?”
“Do you have more than 5 pieces of furniture?”
“Do you own more than 100 things?”
“Does your closet have more than 6 shirts?”
“Why do you own more than 3 books?”
“Why aren’t your rooms painted white?”

This contradicts the functionality of minimalism. Radical online standards of 3-piece wardrobes and bleak Scandinavian interiors have led to the destruction of function. Embracing this deindividualization is a one-way street from ‘too much’ to ‘too little.’ The idea that minimalism looks a certain way, that it can be copied like a recipe, is harmful.

Minimalism doesn’t replace consumerism– it changes the flavor. Self identifying ‘minimalists’ purchase ‘minimal’ products, and the free market is only too happy to supply.

Online minimalism and its siren call of simplicity and happiness and Zen has led to an epidemic. Too many fall into the trap of using minimalism as medicine. Displacing their anxieties and conflicts onto their possessions. Reducing what they own instead of addressing their problems.

Minimize friends, minimize food, minimize furniture, minimize fashion, minimize finances, and these are just the F’s. At a certain point you are no longer improving your life, you are gutting it.

Minimalism is focusing your life, it’s an allocation of attention, It’s realizing the relativity of less.
Minimalism isn’t a hashtag, it isn’t a style, it isn’t a number.

how to break up with your phone

May 16th, 2018.

I recommend that everyone read, How to Break Up with Your Phone, by Catherine Price. What an eye opening book. The average American consumer spends five (5!) hours a day on their phone. If that statistic doesn’t worry you, it should.

Excessive usage rewires your brain, and creates chemical imbalances. Not to mention the amount of productivity loss from constant notifications, or the cervical damage from constantly hunching over your phone, or the amount of money wasted on apps that are rarely used.

The problem is that phone usage is a subtle and widespread addiction. Without realizing it you may reach for your phone several hundred times a day. The world around us, the apps themselves, our own brain chemistry, all encourage this continual “grazing” on our phones.

Controlling your phone usage isn’t a silver bullet to reach nirvana, but in an evermore tired and depressed society, it may be what you need.
