Laziness & Fear

July 10, 2018

I got frustrated this week over my naval academy application.
The online form plays out like an ad-lib for secondary school achievement: “My favorite varsity team was (blank), I was well liked and my class’s (stu gov role), between practice and church service I serve my community by (volunteer experience).

It’s not the fault of the application, or of the academy. It does a great job of recognizing the effort and hard work that goes into achievement. The reason it twists my stomach is because it pulls emphasis away from my back-breaking (literally. See my powerlifting training) college experience and focuses on my less-than-stellar High School performance.

See, I was your typical gifted but lazy High Schooler (a laziness which did not extend to my full-time-part-time job washing dishes). I had double digit absences per annum, slept through AP Bio, and got a 5 on my AP Language and Composition test without bothering to study. I remember walking into school one day (late) to discover that the SAT was being given, I borrowed a pencil, and landed in the 95th national percentile. I can safely say that I graduated High School without ever conjugating ‘to study’ into the present or past tense.

Misconception

I don’t believe I was (or am) truly lazy. I believe that laziness is just a symptom of something deeper. Laziness is the visible tip of a much deeper iceberg.

I was afraid of failure.

Let’s put aside the obvious: of course a component of the ‘lazy-but-gifted’ complex is the total lack of good habits to build success upon. No organizational skill. No self-disciple. No intra or extra motivation to accomplish school work. No study habits. No idea how to study. Messy or nonexistent notetaking. Poor attendance. Etc etc. This is all epidermal stuff.

When you’re young and gifted, you don’t fail, then you grow older, you head off to middle school and you still can’t fail- but things start to feel tougher, some days the answers aren’t in your head by the time the teacher finishes talking, and now you’re in High School, and there’s a possibility of failing.

I don’t mean like “fail the class” failing. No, I’m talking about trying and failing. What if I put my head down, worked as hard as I could on a project and it only got a 90. Is my best a 90? That’s a frightening prospect. The paralyzing fear here is to pour your gifted self into something, to try, to put effort in, to labor over something, only to find out that you failed to do well.

It isn’t laziness. It’s just much easier to say: “Oh yeah, that calc test? Got an 87. Never even studied though.” Than it is to say: “That calc test? I was studying for weeks, but I only got a 94.”

You are totally frozen. To melt and pour yourself into something means risking disappointment. And so you hide in the shadow of that numerical gap between your achievement and what your achievement could be (the upper bound of which is unknown).

This position means settling, rather than striving. To be happy with less, because you know it is below you. You know (and everyone else too) that if you tried harder you would do better, but trying harder would reveal your real limit, and your fear of that limit keeps you frozen.

It’s such a deceptively comfortable positon, like the sweetness of the venus fly trap.

Simple Syntax

I’ve been doing a lot of introspection. It’s probably because of the 2+ hour commutes I do daily, or the constant state of brain anesthetic which comes with a tedious job.

I’ve come to realize that all those little phrases, the ones spewed by school staff and repeated ad nauseam, have a lot more meaning than you thought they did when you initially heard and ignored them. Phrases that sound so stupid and shallow like: “Don’t be afraid to fail” or “try your best”, actually can mean a lot.

The biggest irony is that a fear of failure has led me to more failure than I could have ever predicted. But I’m done with it. I’ll blowtorch every icy appendage, I’ll pour every cubic mL into my SAT retake, into my fitness test, into my application, no holding back, no buts, no escape clause. No fear.

I’m going to find that upper bound.
